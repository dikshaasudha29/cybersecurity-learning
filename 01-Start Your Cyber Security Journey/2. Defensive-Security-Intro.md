# Task 1 – Introduction

## Objective

- Introduce Defensive Security fundamentals and operational paradigms.
- Define the role of a Security Operations Center (SOC) in corporate infrastructure defense.

## Key Concepts

- Defensive Security: A reactive and protective strategy focused on monitoring networks, mitigating ongoing threats, and hardening infrastructure.
- Security Operations Center (SOC): A centralized hub where security teams monitor, detect, analyze, and respond to cyber incidents in real time.
- Incident Response: The structured workflow used by defenders to contain and eliminate a live attack.

## What I Learned

- Defending an organization requires a structured team approach, working inside a centralized unit like a SOC.
- Defensive security focuses on identifying ongoing malicious activity and stopping it before it compromises critical assets
- Cybersecurity is a continuous loop: the offensive flaws found in 'Offensive-Security-Intro' room are what defenders actively watch for and prevent in this room.

# Task 2 – Phase 1: An Attack Begins

## Objective

- Perform real-time security log triage and alert analysis.
- Identify indicators of malicious authentication activity via a centralized monitoring console.

## Key Concepts

- Security Monitoring & Triage: The process of continuously reviewing alerts generated by security dashboards to separate benign activities from true threats.
- Brute-Force Authentication Attempt: An adversarial attack vector where automated inputs generate rapid, repeated authentication requests to guess account credentials.
- Audit Logging & Telemetry: The historical tracking of user events, network traffic, and system behaviors across an enterprise infrastructure.

## Tools & Methods Used

- SIEM / Monitoring Dashboard: Interacting with centralized log visualizers to view real-world telemetry feeds and incoming infrastructure alerts.
- Alert Analysis: Inspecting log details (such as targeted identifiers or timestamps) to deduce the nature and target of an ongoing incident.

## What I Learned

- How to use a security dashboard to maintain situational awareness over an enterprise environment during a live incident.
- How high-volume, failed authentication attempts manifest as automated alerts within a Security Operations Center (SOC) workflow.
- The workflow step of isolating a specific security alert to pull relevant, actionable telemetry for incident response documentation.

# Task 3 – Phase 2: Stopping the Attack

## Objective

- Implement immediate incident containment strategies during an active attack.
- Mitigate active authentication threats by managing system user states.

## Key Concepts

- Incident Containment: The crucial phase of incident response focused on limiting the damage of a live breach and preventing further systemic exposure.
- Account Lockout Control: A defensive mechanism that disables authentication capabilities for a targeted user identifier to neutralize ongoing brute-force cracking attempts.
- Identity and Access Management (IAM) Triage: Modifying live user account privileges or operational status via administrative tools to enforce perimeter safety.

## Tools & Methods Used

- Account Management Console: Utilizing centralized administrative interfaces to govern user directories and change permission profiles.
- Administrative Account Suspension: Manually disabling access tokens or account states to sever the attacker's interactive feedback loop.


## What I Learned

- Speed is vital during an incident to isolate a threat before an adversary shifts tactics or gains deep footholds.
- How temporary account suspension acts as an effective, immediate firewall against high-velocity password guessing patterns.
- The trade-offs of containment: locking an account stops the attacker, but it temporarily disrupts the legitimate user's availability.

## Notes

- Automated vs. Manual Control: While manual lockouts are used during emergency triage, production environments should enforce automated Account Lockout Policies (e.g., locking access after 5 failed tries).
- Blast Radius Reduction: Containment rules must be strict enough to completely neutralize the immediate threat without accidentally knocking down unrelated network subsystems.

#  Task 4 – Phase 3: Investigating the Attacker

## Objective

- Develop and update actionable Cyber Threat Intelligence (CTI) records.
- Document adversarial behavioral indicators to fortify long-term organizational defenses.

## Key Concepts

- Cyber Threat Intelligence (CTI): The collection, analysis, and sharing of information regarding threat actors, their motivations, and their specific attack infrastructure.
- Tactics, Techniques, and Procedures (TTPs): The structural patterns and behavioral footprints that define how a specific adversarial group operates.
- Threat Actor Profiling: Cataloging active threat organizations to better predict their future targets, strategies, and next logical escalation paths.

## Tools & Methods Used

- Threat Intelligence Management System: Interacting with database registries used to log, track, and correlate active threat group behaviors.
- Incident Post-Mortem Documentation: Formally updating internal indicators (such as targeted routes and impacted assets) following immediate threat containment.

## What I Learned

- Defensive security does not stop at containment; analyzing who attacked and how they attacked prevents future incidents.
- Sharing threat data within the global cybersecurity community allows multiple organizations to defend against the same threat group simultaneously.
- Updating internal intelligence records changes defense from a purely reactive response into a proactive, intelligence-driven architecture.

#  Task 5 – Phase 4: Submitting Your Report

## Objective

- Draft a comprehensive Incident Report to close out the security event lifecycle.
- Consolidate technical logs and timeline data into a structured executive summary.

## Key Concepts

- Incident Documentation: The formal process of recording the chronological timeline, impact, and mitigation steps of a security breach.
- Lessons Learned / Post-Mortem: Using the historical data from a closed incident as a blueprint to train staff and patch organizational weaknesses.
- Compliance & Auditing: Maintaining standardized reports (e.g., using ticketing schemas like SOC-YEAR-ID) to meet legal, regulatory, and corporate tracking guidelines.

## Tools & Methods Used

- Incident Reporting Interface: Inputting validated threat parameters (such as the target profile and the affected asset paths) into a tracking console.
- Case Management Systems: Submitting formalized tickets to transition an incident from an "Active Mitigation" status to a "Closed/Resolved" status.

## What I Learned

- An incident is not officially over until the documentation is submitted and archived for the organization's reference.
- Incident reports bridge the gap between technical teams (SOC analysts) and business stakeholders by summarizing the exact business impact.
- Standardized naming structures ensure that historical incidents can be easily searched, audited, and referenced during future investigations.

## Notes

- Data Integrity: Incident summaries must contain objective, factual data points extracted directly from system logs, minimizing speculation.
- Operational Feedback Loop: The structural findings in the final report should directly influence and update the organization's disaster recovery and business continuity plans.

---

# Overall Takeaways

- **Continuous Monitoring**: Centralized dashboards are critical to parse raw logging traffic into actionable alerts.
- **Swift Containment**: Rapid asset isolation or account suspension minimizes the blast radius during an active exploit.
- **Reporting Drives Security**: A security event is only closed once formal post-mortem reports update system patches and defense plans.
